üî• Newton's Third Law in Action

"For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction"

This fundamental principle is the heart of rocket propulsion. When hot gases are expelled at high velocity from the rocket nozzle, the rocket experiences an equal and opposite force that propels it forward.

Chemical Propulsion

Chemical rockets burn fuel and oxidizer to create hot, expanding gases. The Space Shuttle's main engines burned liquid hydrogen and oxygen at temperatures over 3,000¬∞C, producing thrust equivalent to 37 million horsepower.

‚ö°

Electric Propulsion

Ion drives use electricity to accelerate charged particles to extremely high velocities. While producing less thrust than chemical rockets, they're incredibly fuel-efficient for deep space missions.

üåç

Escape Velocity

To escape Earth's gravity, objects must reach 11.2 km/s (40,320 km/h). This speed varies for different celestial bodies - the Moon's escape velocity is only 2.4 km/s due to its lower mass.

üõ∞Ô∏è

Orbital Mechanics

Satellites stay in orbit by continuously falling towards Earth while moving forward fast enough to miss it. The International Space Station orbits at 7.66 km/s, completing one orbit every 92 minutes.

üìö History of Rocketry

1232 CE

üèπ Fire Arrows

Chinese military forces first used bamboo tubes filled with gunpowder as rocket-propelled arrows during the Battle of Kai-fung-fu, marking the birth of rocket technology.

1903

üìñ Tsiolkovsky's Theory

Russian scientist Konstantin Tsiolkovsky published "The Exploration of Cosmic Space by Means of Reaction Devices," establishing the theoretical foundation for spaceflight.

1926

üöÄ First Liquid Rocket

Robert Goddard launched the world's first liquid-fueled rocket in Massachusetts, reaching an altitude of 12.5 meters and flying for 2.5 seconds.

1957

üõ∞Ô∏è Sputnik Era

The Soviet Union launched Sputnik 1, the first artificial satellite, beginning the Space Age and demonstrating the power of rocket technology to the world.

1969

üåô Moon Landing

Apollo 11 successfully landed humans on the Moon using the massive Saturn V rocket, the most powerful rocket ever successfully flown at the time.

2020s

üîÑ Reusable Rockets

Companies like SpaceX revolutionized space access with reusable rockets, dramatically reducing launch costs and opening new possibilities for space exploration.

Water Rocket Challenge

Materials: 2L plastic bottle, cork, bicycle pump, water, fins

Objective: Build and launch a water rocket to understand pressure dynamics and Newton's Third Law

Variables to Test: Water volume (1/3 bottle optimal), air pressure, nose cone design, fin configuration

Safety: Always wear safety glasses and launch in open areas away from people

Extensions: Test different balloon shapes, sizes, and release mechanisms for optimal performance

üöÄ Space Industry Careers

üá¶üá∫ Australia's Growing Space Industry

Australia's space sector is rapidly expanding, with the Australian Space Agency establishing ambitious goals for local space capabilities. From satellite manufacturing to mission support, exciting opportunities await the next generation of space professionals.

üîß

Aerospace Engineer

What they do: Design and test aircraft, spacecraft, satellites, and missiles. Work on propulsion systems, aerodynamics, and structural design.

Education pathway: Bachelor of Aerospace Engineering or Mechanical Engineering with aerospace specialization

Average salary: $85,000 - $130,000 AUD annually

üåü

Astrophysicist

What they do: Study the universe, celestial bodies, and space phenomena. Conduct research, analyze data from space missions and telescopes.

Education pathway: Bachelor of Physics or Astronomy, followed by Honours and PhD

Average salary: $70,000 - $120,000 AUD annually

üõ∞Ô∏è

Satellite Systems Engineer

What they do: Design, develop, and maintain satellite communications systems. Work on ground stations and space-based infrastructure.

Education pathway: Bachelor of Electrical Engineering or Telecommunications Engineering

Average salary: $75,000 - $115,000 AUD annually

üéØ

Mission Operations Specialist

What they do: Plan and execute space missions, monitor spacecraft systems, and coordinate with international space agencies.

Education pathway: Bachelor of Engineering, Physics, or Computer Science

Average salary: $80,000 - $125,000 AUD annually

üíª

Space Software Developer

What they do: Create software for spacecraft control, mission planning, data analysis, and ground support systems.

Education pathway: Bachelor of Computer Science, Software Engineering, or related field

Average salary: $70,000 - $110,000 AUD annually

üì°

Ground Systems Technician

What they do: Maintain and operate ground-based equipment that communicates with spacecraft and satellites.

Education pathway: Certificate IV in Electronics or Diploma of Engineering Technology

Average salary: $60,000 - $85,000 AUD annually
